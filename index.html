Development of BRICS in the 21st century

Introduced as a concept in 2001, gradually becoming an entity after 2006, and maturing with South Africa's accession in 2010, BRICS was once seen as the most dynamic and promising emerging economy of the 21st century. So, after nearly 22 years of development, what about the BRICS countries now? Have new differences emerged among the BRICS countries? We will examine these questions in 4 ways.

1. Changes in GDP shares

From 2000 to 2021, the share of BRICS countries' GDP in the world rose from about 10% to 25%. On the other hand, however, the GDP shares of China, Russia and India did increase significantly, but the other two countries' GDP shares did not change significantly. This visually illustrates that there are indeed differences in economic development among the BRICS countries.

2. Changes in population growth rate

All of the BRICS countries showing a year-on-year decline in population growth after 2013. Among them, Russia remains at a negative population growth level year-round, while China is also approaching the critical line. When there is no longer a demographic dividend, it is imperative to develop new industries driven by high technology, which is naturally one of the tasks of the BRICS countries in the future.

3. Changes in industry

After entering the 21st century, industries in all BRICS countries have developed. But China has been way ahead, surpassing the other four BRICS countries not only in terms of total industrial value added, but also in terms of its incremental growth.

4. Changes in price and income

With the gradual economic development, the GNI per cita of BRICS countries also shows a steady growth trend, which reflects the rising living standard of people living in BRICS countries; however, if compared with the five developed countries as shown in the chart, there is still a big gap between BRICS countries and them in this regard. In terms of domestic prices, the growth speed of the consumer price index of BRICS countries is also significantly higher than that of developed countries. This shows that despite some achievements, the BRICS countries still have a long way to go in improving people's livelihood.
